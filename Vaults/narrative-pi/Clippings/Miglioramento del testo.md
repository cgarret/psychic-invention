### Chapter 1: The Intrusion

Mark Mathias Parker sat in the warm cone of the desk lamp, crayons and coloring books scattered across the small table. His hands—shaped by years at a keyboard—moved with quiet precision, shading deep indigo into the intricate loops of a mandala. Each stroke deliberate. Each stroke a small act of peace.

The refrigerator hummed in the kitchen. Its drone wove with the soft scratch of wax on paper, wrapping the room in something close to stillness.

Then, a cluck.

One sound from outside, and the crayon stopped. Time slowed. Mark's eyes dropped to the coloring book—a cheerful yellow chicken beamed up at him, beak curved in what now looked like a grin. His pulse surged, loud and erratic in the quiet room.

He shoved the book away. It skidded to the table's edge. Outside the window, a tawny rooster scratched at the earth, head down, entirely indifferent.

The distant clamor rose in his memory before he could stop it—hens, and beneath the hens his father's voice, warm and authoritative in the same breath, conjuring a childhood that smelled of feathers and damp soil. Mark was on his feet before he knew it. The coffee table went over, its contents scattering across the floor.

"No, no," he whispered.

He crossed the room and slammed the window shut. The glass shook in the frame. He pressed his fists against the pane, holding them there until the clucking faded and the evening settled back into silence.

"I'm sorry," he gasped, his breath fogging the cold glass. He slid down the wall to the floor, trembling hands covering his face, and stayed there among the scattered crayons.

### Chapter 2: The Boy and the Birds

Five-year-old Mark crouched on the kitchen floor, knees pulled to his chest. Outside, roosters crowed as dusk fell. The summer air carried the sour reek of beer soaked into tile, and his father's voice filled the next room—sharp, then louder, then a silence worse than the shouting.

Mark made himself small. He'd learned that early.

On the table above him, a plate of rice and chicken sat mostly untouched. He remembered the first time his father had made him eat it. He'd choked, spat the meat out, and Papa had slapped the plate to the floor. Feathers had been scattered across the linoleum somehow—Mark could never explain that detail. His father had said something in Thai that Mark didn't need to understand to feel its shape: *useless. Weak as a chicken.*

The words had claws.

That afternoon, he'd pressed his ear to the door and caught fragments of his mother's voice on the phone. *Nothing's good enough. I can't stay here.* His father had scoffed, then hurled a bottle at the wall. Mark had watched a shard of glass skate toward his bare foot and held perfectly still.

He knew how the story ended. He'd been one year old, barely walking, when she left. But he'd been told it so many times it felt like memory: dawn light, her jasmine perfume, her leaning over his crib to brush the hair from his forehead. *Be brave.* A kiss. Then the soft click of the front door, which was the loudest sound he'd ever heard.

After that, the dream began. A giant chicken—yellow, coal-eyed, enormous—waited at his bedroom door each night, beak snapping. He woke screaming. His father never came.

### Chapter 3: The Coop

The memory arrived not as an image but as a temperature—the dense, humid heat of a Virginia summer trapped under a tin roof.

Mark was seven, standing in the doorway of the Big Coop.

It was his father's pride: a sprawling wire-mesh enclosure for prize-winning Rhode Island Reds. To the neighbors, his father was a gentle giant who could coax a tomato vine to double its yield. Inside the coop, the air was different.

"Hold the bucket steady, Marky."

Not angry. Just heavy with expectation. The air tasted of ammonia and warm feathers, and dusty shafts of light cut through the slats, beautiful and wrong.

To a seven-year-old, the chickens were not farm animals. Their eyes were unblinking black beads—not cruel, just utterly indifferent. That indifference was worse than cruelty.

"They smell fear, son," his father said, wading into the flock to retrieve a broody hen. "You freeze up, they peck. You gotta move with purpose."

Mark tried. His boots felt poured with lead. He gripped the bucket handle until his knuckles whitened.

The General stopped two feet away—the prize rooster, feathers of burnished copper and oil-slick black—and fixed Mark with one lateral eye.

*Don't look at him. Look at the feed.*

The rooster sensed the hesitation. He launched himself in an explosion of wings. Mark didn't scream. He gasped, and the bucket dropped, and grain went everywhere, and then the whole flock was around him—beaks, claws, a deafening rush of feathers—

His father's arms came in, parting the birds, scooping Mark up and carrying him out into the open air. He set him down on the cool grass. A scratch on Mark's cheek welled with blood. Mark's whole body shook.

He waited.

His father wiped his brow with a rag.

"You gotta toughen up, Marky," he said, voice soft and final. "It's just a bird. If you let a bird scare you, how you gonna handle the world?"

### Chapter 4: The Anchor

Back in the present, Mark sat on the floor, his spine against the sofa. *You gotta toughen up.* The words still moved through him like current.

He squeezed his eyes shut, then forced them open. The room tilted. He needed to count. He needed to find the ground.

"Five things I see." His voice was a rasp. "The blue crayon. The grain of the oak floorboards. The brass lamp base. The coffee stain on the overturned table. My hands."

"Four things I feel. The cold varnish of the wood. The scratchy wool of the rug. The waxy surface of the crayon. The fabric of my shirt."

"Three things I hear. The refrigerator humming. Wind rattling the windowpane. My own heartbeat."

"Two things I smell. Old paper. Coffee."

"One thing I taste. Mint."

He exhaled, long and shuddering. The Big Coop receded. The General was gone. He was just Mark, on a floor, in a room, alive.

### Chapter 5: The Call

Mark stared at the phone. Calling felt like defeat. He pressed Jenny's icon.

"Mark?" Warm. Alert.

"I had an episode," he said. "The neighbor got a rooster. I flipped the coffee table, Jen. A grown man."

"You weren't hiding from a bird," Jenny said. "You were reacting to a memory. There's a difference. Dad never understood the difference between being strong and being numb."

A silence. Then: "You pulled yourself out of it. You grounded yourself. You called me. That's strength, Mark."

"Thanks, Jen," he said, and the knot in his chest loosened, just slightly.

"Call me anytime. Even if it's just to breathe on the phone. You're not in there alone anymore."

### Chapter 6: Canvas and Code

Years passed. Mark grew into a tall, wiry man with startlingly intense eyes. At thirty, he found work as a programmer. It was there he met Shaquille Ryan—Shaq—who loved cartoons and didn't mind Mark's habit of coloring.

"You gotta try this," Shaq grinned one evening, sliding across a book of intricate mandalas.

Mark paused at an illustration of a hen. Shaq caught it.

"Scared of those?"

"It's nothing," Mark said, his heart loud in his chest.

They colored that night, and then again the next week, joined eventually by Heidi Matthews and Bruce Lyons over video call. A chosen family assembled in those quiet sessions. Whenever Mark felt unmoored—missing Mama, rattled by a nightmare—one of them pulled him back. In those hours he almost believed nothing would harm him again.

### Chapter 7: Amber Jealousy

Mark had nearly given up on love when Dana Goodwin came into his life. She was vivacious, sharp, a fellow programmer. He thought he'd gotten lucky.

In the amber light of the café, he watched her exchange a wave with a man at the counter.

"Who's that?" His voice came out flat.

"Carl," she said easily. "From my project team."

Jealousy moved through him like something cold and corrosive. *She'll leave you.* His father's voice, unhelpfully on time.

When Dana came back from paying, Mark said, "Who was that, really? Don't lie to me."

"Mark, please—I love you—"

Something old and cold snapped.

He was across the table before he understood what he was doing. His fist connected with her cheek. He pinned her, hands closing around her throat. He wasn't seeing Dana. He was seeing a suitcase by the front door. He was stopping the leaving.

Dana's body went still. Mark stumbled back. Stared at his hands.

In that quiet corner soaked in amber light, the truth arrived without mercy: he had killed her.

### Chapter 8: Fractured Reflections

A week later, the doorbell rang.

Kirsten and Lucy Parker stood in the hallway. Half-sisters. The Second Family. They looked nervous.

"We know about Dana," Kirsten said. "We wanted to see you."

Mark stared. *The ones Papa loved.* He said nothing.

Lucy sat at his table. She picked up a butter knife—not threatening, just something to hold. "Dad used to butter Mom's toast every morning," she said softly.

The rage came up white and wordless. They were intruders in his coop.

"Get out," he said.

Lucy reached across and touched his cheek. Her fingernail caught the skin. A thin, bright line of pain—like a thorn, like the rooster.

The man vanished. The terrified boy took over.

He shoved Lucy into the counter. He forced Kirsten onto the table and closed his hands around her throat and did not stop until the struggling ceased.

He stood in the silence that followed, touching the scratch on his cheek. In the window's dark glass, a jagged scar stretched from ear to chin. He had done it again.

### Chapter 9: Echoes of Silence

Autumn turned to winter. Mark was sixty-one now, moving through his days like a hollow man, his cruelties collected and organized, quiet as routine.

He met Jennifer Lyra Rowe at a conference. She was gentle—reminded him of Jenny. He thought, briefly, that it wasn't too late.

One night she found him rocking on the kitchen floor, staring at his reflection in the dark face of the refrigerator. His hands were coated in chocolate that had dried the wrong color.

"Mark," she whispered. "What happened to you?" She reached for the scar on his cheek.

He flinched.

"I'm scared of you now," she said.

Jennifer left. The words had been both sentence and verdict.

Shaq called that night. Mark answered and said nothing for a long time.

"They're all gone," he finally said. "Not a single one left."

### Chapter 10: The Grand Illusion

Morning brought no light. Only revelation.

Mark lay in bed. The ceiling fan turned above him— *thwup, thwup, thwup* —and the sound transformed without warning into the beating of wings.

He sat up. The apartment was silent and deafening. He went to the window.

The neighbor's yard was empty. And yet—

The General stood in the center of the lawn. Impossibly large, feathers burning with the iridescence of oil and blood, not looking at the ground. Looking up. At him.

Mark backed away, hit his work table. Crayons scattered—Indigo, Scarlet, Canary Yellow. He picked up the yellow one. He ripped open his sketchbook.

"If I draw it, I control it," he said, his voice a cracked reed. "I am the designer."

His hand moved fast. He drew the beak, sharp and deliberate. He drew the talons. But the wax wouldn't stay on the paper. The yellow seemed to bleed from the page, dripping down his fingers, coating his hands in a feverish, jaundiced light.

*Cluck.*

The sound was inside the room.

Mark turned toward the kitchen.

The chicken sat on the overturned coffee table. It had Dana's eyes. It had Lucy's hair.

"You gotta toughen up, Marky," it said, in his father's voice.

Mark hit the wall. The drywall began to peel away around him, opening into wire mesh, and the soothing cocoon of his apartment dissolved into the Big Coop. He had never left. He saw that now.

The air filled with ammonia and old blood.

"I'm sorry," Mark wept, curling small. "I'm sorry, Jenny. I'm sorry."

Jenny wasn't there. The flock emerged from the shadows—Dana, Kirsten, Lucy, Heidi, Bruce—all wearing the masks of birds, heads cocking in perfect unison.

The General stepped forward. It did not peck. It opened its beak and laughed: a sound of pure, unraveling madness.

Mark reached for the Indigo crayon. He didn't try to draw. He raised it like a dagger—not against the birds, but against the memory.

"Be brave," he whispered to the empty, crowded room.

And then, the feathers descended.
